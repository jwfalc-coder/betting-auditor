### F001 — paper_tracker.py NameError (most urgent)

The 10:00 run produced a Python traceback at paper_tracker.py:156 — `NameError: name 'high_odds_min_edge' is not defined`. This is a hard crash inside `log_paper_trades()`. Code audit AUDIT-001 (generated 09:12 today, ~48 minutes before this run) described the `high_odds_min_edge` variable as still being assigned via a config.get() fallback. The actual crash shows the assignment was removed between audit generation and the run, while the conditional at line 156 was not. This is a half-applied fix. The paper tracker is crashing on every run that hits this code path. The exit code of the overall routine is still 0 (the shell script reports success), masking the failure. Impact: unknown number of paper trades may be going unlogged, and the paper_tracker.json state post-crash is unverified.

**This is the most urgent finding. Fix: remove the dangling condition at paper_tracker.py:156 per AUDIT-001's proposed_fix, then verify paper_tracker.json integrity.**

---

### F002 — Post-election political pipeline empty; unsanctioned sports pivot

The May 7 UK local elections have fully resolved. The Betfair political catalogue is now near-empty, and today's 10:00 run found **zero** political markets (down from the council election batch that resolved on 2026-05-08). The bot's fallback is 15 Matchbook sports markets (Winner, Match Odds, Top Finish, Correct Score). This is a significant domain shift: the edge model was developed and validated on UK political prediction markets, where binary outcomes (Majority Control? Yes/No) are well-defined and Betfair offers deep liquidity. Sports markets have different overround structures, resolution mechanisms, and market-maker sophistication.

All recent Matchbook losses are sports back bets — the very market type the model was not designed for. External research confirms that Matchbook has sufficient sports liquidity but that its sports pricing is less efficient than Betfair's. The risk is that the bot is generating signals in a domain where its edge estimate is unreliable, while the paper_tracker crash means those signals may not even be correctly recorded.

The next confirmed Betfair political market with active odds is the Starmer leadership departure market (Betfair pricing Starmer to leave Jul-Sep 2026 at 2.42). UK by-elections or any leadership elections could provide a re-entry point.

### F004 — Back bets: 20.0% win rate (n=10), -£31.96

All 10 back bet trades are losing at 20.0% win rate. The favourite-longshot bias literature (Whelan 2024; NBER w15923) provides a structural explanation: exchange markets systematically disadvantage backers relative to layers because bettors overweight small probabilities, creating inflated longshot odds that backers pay into. At 10-15pp apparent edge, the model may not be clearing the threshold needed to overcome this structural bias. A binomial calculation: P(≤2 wins from 10 trials | true rate = 0.5) ≈ 5.5%, which would ordinarily suggest the 20% win rate is a real signal — but the signal here is likely "wrong domain" (sports) rather than a fundamental model flaw for political lays.

---

### F005 — 10-11pp band: 50.0% win rate (n=16), -£57.62

No new trades in this band since yesterday. The data remain unchanged but the external research on noise traders in prediction markets (REPEC working paper on Betfair liquidity and efficiency) supports the hypothesis that 10-11pp apparent edge in lower-liquidity political markets is within the model's error bounds. The 7-9pp distribution data (33.3% win rate on 27 resolved observations) further supports a noise floor well above 9pp. Raising the trade threshold to 12pp appears justified by the data: it would have excluded £57.62 in losses while preserving £154.94 in gains.
