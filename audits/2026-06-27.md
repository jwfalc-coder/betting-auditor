## Audit narrative — 2026-06-27

### Context

This is the first audit covering the new **lay-only regime** (MOD-280/281, deployed 2026-06-27). The strategy change is evidence-based: a 292-trade audit found lays are the sole profit engine (155 trades, 69% WR, 44% ROI; backtest 79%/53%) while backs were structurally break-even. Today is the first operational day under the new rules, which makes pipeline conformance the primary audit focus.

### Pipeline health

Six genuine scheduled runs completed cleanly. The new 1230 slot (F001) is missing on its inaugural day — the most likely explanation is a cron registration gap rather than a mid-run crash (no partial log exists). The 0001 carryover log (00:01 BST, tail of 2026-06-26) is legitimate schedule overlap and not a problem.

Provider health was mostly good: Gemini 503 errors occurred in the 0600 and 2200 runs but recovered successfully (Claude Code fallback and retry respectively). No daily-cap 429 on Gemini — a notable improvement over the 2026-06-22 audit (F001 that cycle). CricketData API failures appeared in the three evening runs but did not block signal generation.

### Lay-only transition — conformance and anomaly

MOD-280 correctly suppressed the outright BACKs it was designed to catch (Tommy Fleetwood Top 10, Naomi Osaka WTA Winner, 4× ATP Eastbourne Winners in the 1030 run). However, four Match Odds BACKs in the 75–77% implied probability range logged as new trades despite falling squarely in the MOD-281 high_prob suppression zone (F002). All four are also sub-10pp edge (5.37–9.43pp). The most likely explanation is that MOD-281 disabled the high_prob/near_certainty track as a named code path, but standard Match Odds evaluations routing through a different path at the same probability level were not caught. The operator should verify whether these appeared in the Telegram slip as suppressed or live recommendations before deciding if they should have been placed.

### External research — lay-fade edge sustainability

Research confirms the favourite-longshot bias (FLB) is persistent but weaker on exchanges than in traditional bookmaking. A 2025 community discussion ("Has Betfair Developed a Fav' Longshot Bias?") noted the phenomenon may be evolving on Betfair as the exchange matures, but the consensus in academic literature is that it survives on exchanges in niche/outright markets where information is asymmetric and liquidity is thin. For a keyword-based favourite-fade model operating on multi-runner outrights and Match Odds, the FLB provides structural support for the lay thesis — overpriced favourites in thin markets are exactly where the bias is most persistent.

Tennis outright winner markets also show systematic favourite overpricing: heavy favourites (priced ≤ 1.60) have been found to win 5–12pp below implied probability over a decade of data. This supports the non-golf outright lay sourcing strategy, provided liquidity allows execution.

### External research — lay liquidity

The liquidity picture for the bot's target markets is constrained. General expert consensus (2026): outside EPL, cricket, and major leagues, Betfair liquidity is thin, spreads are wide, and fills above trivial size are difficult. Politics specials and niche outright markets are the thinnest. The current £20–50 per-bet lay liability cap is appropriate risk management given this environment, but the operator should expect partial matching rather than full fill on thin markets (golf Top-N outrights, political specials). Pre-event windows and final-round/pre-market surge points are where outright lay liquidity is best on exchanges.

### Betfair platform update

Betfair replaced its Premium Charge with an Expert Fee in January 2025 (tiers: 0% under £25k annual gross profit, 20% on £25k–£100k, 40% above £100k — capped at 40% vs. former 60%). Additionally, "My Betfair Rewards" now allows UK/IRE customers to choose between 2%, 5%, or 8% commission on the exchange. Both changes are immaterial at the bot's current scale (balance £115.95) but the 2%/5%/8% commission choice is worth confirming — if the bot defaults to 5%, qualifying for the 2% tier could reduce trade costs once volume grows.
